Excuse this non-science-related tangent! With the chaos, uncertainty, and transition that marked the end of 2020 and beginning of 2021, I picked up "A Higher Loyalty" by former FBI director James Comey hoping to do more than reflect on some sordid stories of the outgoing administration. I was curious to see how Comey's treatise on ethical leadership lined up with our country's new administration, how his admonitions about the Trump administration resonate 3 years later, and whether Comey had any wise points of light to offer in an increasingly dark, chaotic political milieu.

I found Comey's account of the culture of Rudy Giuliani's office, in which he worked as a young prosecutor in the late 1980's, insightful and timely: *"Though Giuliani’s confidence was exciting, it fed an imperial style that severely narrowed the circle of people with whom he interacted, something I didn’t realize was dangerous until much later: a leader needs the truth, but an emperor does not consistently hear it from his underlings."* In this quote lies one of the cruxes of Comey's thesis, that the fundamental basis of ethical leadership is the truth – speaking the truth, hearing the truth, and creating a culture that speaks truth to power. This passage made me reflect how the internet may be neutering our society's ability to create ethical leaders by fraying the fabric of truth and creatiing a online outrage culture that actually fails to speak truth to true centers of power (elected officials, social media corporations, etc).

Another fascinating excerpt from Comey's book concerned the legality of many of the federal government's "interrogation" methods post-9/11. Comey singles out the **confirmation bias** of CIA leadership, their inability to see beyond the world of facts that supported their position that their extreme interrogation tactics were legally and morally justified. Comey highlights that our confirmation bias is not only biologically hardwired, but also exacerbated by a political culture that views indecision as weakness. Comey contends that doubt should be seen as a sign of wisdom and humility, as the toughest decisions come with the axiomatic possibility of being wrong. The rigidity of thought in today's polarized political climate is concerning to me: how doubt is seen as weakness, how designation as "Democrat" or "Republican" all but determines how constituents and politicians rule on important nuanced issues, and how our penchant for building echo chambers is biologically, politically, and algorithmically ingrained in our society.

What struck Comey most about President Obama was his "ability to focus" and his sense of humor. According to Comey: *"Having a balance of confidence and humility is
essential to effective leadership."* What surprised me the most was the way he highlighted laughter as a mark of a strong leader: *"Laughing in a genuine way requires a certain
level of confidence, because we all look a little silly laughing."* In Comey's estimation, confidence and humility aren't competing qualities, but are self-reinforcing forces that govern one's leadership abilities.

Personally, I have difficulty having "tough conversations" with others. Comey made me reflect on the importance of these tough conversations in leadership settings: *"By avoiding hard conversations and not telling people where they were struggling and how they could improve, I was depriving them of the chance to grow."* I will try and carry and use this nuggest of wisdom in my own life, by recognizing that "difficult conversations" are all about honesty and growth.

Comey also spoke to one of President Obama's greatest strengths: his capacity to listen and process information from others. He cites the "Washington Listen" as a ubiquitous feature of political discourse, where people wait to speak rather than hear what others say. But, according to Comey, President Obama didn't just tolerate others perspectives, he actively sought out and encouraged all viewpoints. This humble, cooperative ethos is best encapsulated by the question President Obama often asked: "what am I missing?".

Perhaps Comey's most insightful point lies in differentiating intelligence and judgement: *"Intelligence is the ability to solve a problem, to decipher a riddle, to master a set of facts. Judgment is the ability to orbit a problem or a set of facts and see it as it might be seen through other eyes"*. Thus, there is nothing inherently ethical about intelligence, while judgement requires a more thoughful, empathetic engagement with the objects that which intelligence are concerned with.
